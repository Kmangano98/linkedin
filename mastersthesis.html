My thesis explores the interpretability of Knowledge-Primed Neural Networks (KPNNs) using single-cell CRISPR perturbation screens. The goal is to evaluate whether these neural networks can capture biologically meaningful signals when trained to distinguish cell types or stimulation responses.

I benchmark the network's feature importance against known ground truth data from large-scale perturbation experiments. This includes analyzing transcription factor activity, correcting model bias, and validating enrichment using gene regulatory networks.

In doing so, I aim to advance the understanding of how machine learning models interpret cellular state and to contribute to transparent, reproducible tools in computational biology.

This project combines my interests in bioinformatics, machine learning, and functional genomics, and reflects my drive to apply programming skills to solve real biological problems.
